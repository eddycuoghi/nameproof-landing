Generation is easy. Validation is the hard part.

In 2026, generating startup name ideas costs nothing. You can ask any AI assistant for fifty names in thirty seconds, and most of them will sound reasonable. The problem is not finding a name that sounds good. The problem is knowing whether a name is actually safe to build a business around.

A name that sounds distinctive might already be trademarked in your industry. A name that feels modern might collide with an established brand in search results, making it nearly impossible to build organic visibility from day one. A name that passes a quick Google search might have a social handle conflict that forces you to use an inconsistent username across every platform.

The question is never "does this name sound good?" The question is "can I safely build my business around this name?"

What an AI name generator cannot tell you

An AI name generator — including the best ones — produces names based on linguistic patterns, aesthetics and your brief. That is genuinely useful for inspiration. But it cannot tell you whether the name you like has a registered trademark in Class 35, whether the .com is parked by a broker, whether a LinkedIn company page already exists under that name, or whether the name will perform in search results against entrenched competitors.

These are not small details. They are the difference between a name you can build on and a name that will cost you a rebrand six months after launch.

What NameProof does instead

NameProof is not a name generator. It is a decision tool. You bring the name you have already chosen — or the shortlist you have created — and NameProof tells you whether it is safe to move forward.

The NameProof Decision Framework evaluates your name across eight signals: domain availability, social handles, SEO collision analysis, trademark screening across USPTO, EUIPO and WIPO, Company & Registry Signals via Companies House and UKIPO, post-Brexit conflict analysis, and linguistic and pronunciation review. Every Brand Decision Report ends with a single, clear recommendation: Recommended, Recommended with Caution, or Not Recommended.
